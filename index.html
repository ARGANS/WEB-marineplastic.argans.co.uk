Monitoring Marine Plastics from Space

Since 2000 the global production of plastic has risen from 200 to 380 million metric tons

By 2040 the volume of plastic flowing into marine areas is forecast to triple, adding 27-37 million metric tons of plastic per annum ‐ UNEP 2021

Plastics accumulate in floating mats of debris along ocean fronts and are visual testimony to the scale of problem

Photo by Caroline Power, @carolinepower/photography

90% of marine plastic is less than 5mm in size. These microplastics aggregate within oceanic gyres to form a non-biodegradable toxic 'plastic soup' that enters the aquatic food chain with detrimental consequences to biodiversity and human health

Earth Observation (EO) satellite remote sensing provides the means to observe phenomena at the global scale and the challenge is to develop algorithms and sensors optimized to the role of detecting and monitoring marine plastics

ARGANS Marine Litter Program

Since 2017 ARGANS has been commissioned by the European Space Agency and European Commission to undertake R&D projects to develop methods & techniques for detecting and monitoring marine plastics and applying these techniques to case studies in the North Eastern Atlantic, the Mediterranean, and the Black Sea.

RESMALI

Feasibility of Plastic Detection from Space

Marine plastics, absorb and scatter light according to characteristic optical properties due to the polymers and dyes used in manufacture and, in principle, a distinct spectral signature could be detected by remote sensing radiometers.

Under the General Studies Programme (GSP), the European Space Agency (ESA) initiated in 2017 the project 'Remote Sensing of Marine Litter' by funding two parallel contracts - RESMALI, led by ARGANS Limited, UK (team including: the University of Cadiz, Airbus and The Ocean Cleanup) and OPTIMAL, led by the Plymouth Marine Laboratory, UK.

The objective of RESMALI was to perform field sampling, experiments and modelling to determine the capability to detect plastic ML from orbiting sensors. A comprehensive SWIR spectral signature library was compiled from virgin and oceanic plastics, acquired for different concentrations and kinds of plastics in varying stages of degradation, both wet and dry and some collected and frozen to preserve the biofilm that builds up on ML over time and also contributes to the spectral signature.

This library provided spectral measurements used as proxies for BOA water-leaving reflectance, which was then attenuated by applying RTMs that model the effect that the atmosphere has on light propagation depending on the gaseous composition, water vapour and aerosol properties to infer the signal at TOA.

A paper published in the Journal of Hazardous Materials presents the findings of the study, which identified spectral signatures with a range of wavelengths from visible to SWIR that have affordable SNR that the impact on the TOA signal, especially at viewing angles in the direction of sunglint, would enable detection and estimates of coverage from space down to 1% surface concentration. Further information about this project can be found by following The Ocean Cleanup.

EOTrack Med

EO Tracking of Marine Debris in the Mediterranean Sea

This project focuses on the Mediterranean basin, and combines public or professional crowdsourcing alert systems; monitoring by remote sensors; and hydrodynamic models to build up a service for monitoring marine pollution using sensors already in orbit, specifically with data from HR Sentinel and commercial data from VHR.

ML-OPSI

Marine Litter - Operations Performance Simulator

By providing a modelling & simulation framework enabling researchers to build virtual experiments by combining pre-built, re-useable, building blocks within a low-code environment and thus, by democratization, reduce the barriers to entry for using the various sophisticated computational techniques available for investigating and understanding complex systems, like monitoring marine plastic from satellite.
